What is Humanism?

Humanism is a worldview that centers on human beings (their experiences, capacities and responsibilities) as the foundation for understanding life and making ethical choices. It affirms that humans possess inherent worth and that meaning, morality and knowledge arise from human reason, creativity and concern for others.
The roots of Humanism can be traced to ancient Greece and Rome, where philosophers such as Socrates and Aristotle emphasized reason and ethics grounded in human nature. These ideas reemerged powerfully during the Renaissance, when thinkers argued that education, critical inquiry and engagement with art and literature could improve both individuals and society. This historical Humanism focused on developing human potential rather than preparing for an afterlife.
Contemporary Humanism is typically secular and naturalistic. It relies on Science as the best method for understanding the Universe and rejects supernatural explanations as necessary for morality or purpose. Humanists believe ethical values should be based on human well-being, using empathy, Logic and evidence to determine what helps individuals and communities thrive.
Another defining feature of Humanism is personal and social responsibility. Because meaning is not handed down by external authorities, humans must take responsibility for their choices and their impact on others. This often leads Humanists to support ideals such as freedom of thought, equality, human rights and democratic decision-making.
Overall, Humanism is not a rigid doctrine, but a flexible approach to life. It encourages people to think critically, act compassionately and work toward a world in which human dignity and flourishing are central goals.
